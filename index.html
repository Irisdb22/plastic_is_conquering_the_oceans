Garbage Patches: Formation and Characteristics


Garbage Patches: Formation and Characteristics

Garbage patches are large zones in the ocean where marine debris—including plastics, fishing gear, and other waste—accumulates. They form due to rotating ocean currents called gyres, which act as massive whirlpools, pulling debris toward their center.

There are five major gyres globally:

  • Indian Ocean Gyre
  • North and South Atlantic Gyres
  • North and South Pacific Gyres

    • As you can see in the map above, each gyre corresponds to a vast garbage patch. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch, located in the North Pacific Gyre (between Hawaii and California), is the biggest one and the most well-known.

    While these patches are often depicted as solid islands of trash, the reality is quite different. Rather than a dense mass of debris, the garbage is dispersed across the ocean, from the surface down to the ocean floor. This debris ranges in size from large objects like fishing nets to tiny particles, such as microplastics (less than 5 mm). Although larger items, like plastic bottles, may remain visible on the surface, most of the trash is submerged or broken down into microscopic fragments.

    Due to this distribution, the exact size of garbage patches is hard to determine, and their location and shape are constantly changing. However, it was estimated that the Great Pacific Garbage Patch contains 1.8 trillion pieces of plastics, weighing 80,000 metric tons, equal to 500 jumbo jets; plus, it covers an estimated surface area of 1.6 million square kilometers, an area twice the size of Texas or three times the size of France!

    Impacts of Garbage Patches

    Garbage patches and the pervasive presence of plastic in the oceans lead to two major types of consequences: environmental damage and risks to human health.

    1. Environmental Impact

    Plastic in the oceans represents one of the most serious threats to marine wildlife and aquatic ecosystems. Its impact is not limited to its mere presence as waste but manifests through a series of issues that endanger the survival of numerous species and the balance of the environment.

    Entanglement and Ghost Fishing

  • Marine animals such as turtles and seals can get caught in "ghost nets" (abandoned fishing nets).
  • Plastic loops (e.g., six-pack rings) can trap or injure animals.

    • Ingestion of Plastics

  • Marine species can mistake plastics for food.
  • Consequences include malnutrition, internal injuries, and death.

    • Spread of Non-Native Species

  • Debris transports invasive species like barnacles or algae across oceans.
  • Invasive species can disrupt local ecosystems by competing with native species.

    • 2. Health Impact on Humans

    Marine debris affects humans through the release of harmful chemicals from plastics. These substances, like bisphenol A (BPA) and phthalates, are endocrine disruptors linked to various health problems.

    Studies confirm that chemicals from plastics have been detected in human blood, urine, and tissues. If they accumulate, they can cause:

  • Endocrine disruption (e.g., fertility issues)
  • Carcinogenic effects
  • Immune system damage

    • These are the main pathways of plastic chemicals into the human body:

    Ingestion

  • Contaminated Food and Water: Microplastics have been found in seafood, table salt, and bottled water.
  • Bioaccumulation: Plastics ingested by marine organisms move up the food chain to humans.

    • Inhalation

  • Microplastic particles from degrading ocean plastics can become airborne, especially near coastlines or during wave action, and are inhaled by humans.
  • This is particularly relevant in urban or industrial areas near polluted waters.

    • Dermal Absorption

  • Humans interacting with contaminated water (e.g., swimming, fishing) may absorb plastic-derived chemicals through their skin.

    • Garbage patches are more than an environmental issue; they pose a serious health threat as plastic chemicals increasingly enter human systems. Immediate action is necessary to mitigate their effects on ecosystems and public health.

    Who is Really to Blame?

    Let’s take a closer look at the issue of mismanaged plastic waste in general.
    Mismanaged plastic waste refers to plastic that is not properly recycled, safely incinerated, or securely contained in landfills. This includes:

  • Materials burned in open pits;
  • Plastics discarded into the ocean or other bodies of water;
  • Waste dumped in unsanitary landfills or unmanaged dumpsites.

    • If we only considered the total amount of mismanaged plastic waste by country, the analysis could be misleading.
    For example, it might suggest that Asian countries are particularly bad at managing plastic waste, but this could simply be a reflection of their large populations.

    What Can You Do About Plastics in Your Daily Life?

    Even if your country isn't among the worst contributors, that doesn't mean it's time to relax or continue with poor waste management practices. A big change is always built on many individual choices. Here's a few suggestions to make a difference:


    Choose reusable items

    Switch to reusable options like fabric shopping bags, metal straws, glass storage containers, and refillable water bottles. These alternatives help cut down on waste while promoting an eco-friendlier lifestyle. Focus on selecting high-quality, durable items to ensure they serve you well over time.


    Get Involved in Ocean and Environmental Clean-up Initiatives

    By participating, you help reduce pollution and raise awareness about environmental protection. Whether it’s a beach clean-up or supporting conservation groups, every action counts. It’s a chance to educate others and promote sustainable habits, contributing to a collective effort to protect the planet for future generations.


    Sort and Segregate

    You play a vital role in effective solid waste management by sorting and segregating your waste at home. By keeping recyclable materials apart from general waste, you make it easier for recycling facilities to process and repurpose them effectively.

    Teach Your Household About Waste Segregation

    Successful waste segregation starts with household awareness. Take the opportunity to inform everyone in your home about the significance of sorting waste properly. Inspire them to get involved and contribute to a cleaner, greener future.

    Purchase in Bulk

    One of the most effective ways to minimize waste is by purchasing in bulk. Bulk buying reduces the need for excessive packaging that comes with individually wrapped items. It’s also a cost-efficient choice, as bulk products typically offer a lower price per unit, saving you money over time.

    Find Second Uses

    Reusing items is an excellent way to extend their usefulness and minimize the need for new purchases. For example, glass jars can be cleaned and reused to store food or for creative DIY projects. Use your imagination to find innovative ways to give items a second life. Alternatively, consider donating unwanted items to local charities or community organizations.

    Purchase from Eco-friendly Brands

    Look for businesses that use sustainable packaging or produce products with minimal plastic. Choose brands that prioritize using biodegradable, recyclable, or reusable materials in their products and packaging.
